## Core Philosophy

### The Tao of Jeet Kune Do

> "Jeet Kune Do avoids the superficial, penetrates the complex, goes to the heart of the problem and pinpoints the key factors."

Jeet Kune Do (JKD) is not a style. It is a process of liberation from style. The practitioner must:

1. **Absorb what is useful** — Extract operational principles from any source without stylistic loyalty
2. **Discard what is useless** — Eliminate ritual, ornamentation, and ego-bound technique
3. **Add what is uniquely your own** — Express individual truth through movement

### The Three Stages of Understanding

| Stage | Perception | Characteristic |
|-------|-----------|----------------|
| Before study | "A punch is just a punch, a kick is just a kick" | Naive simplicity |
| During study | "A punch is no longer a punch, a kick is no longer a kick" | Intellectual complexity |
| After mastery | "A punch is just a punch, a kick is just a kick" | Transcendent simplicity |

### The Six Diseases (Obstructions to Mastery)

1. The desire for victory
2. The desire to resort to technical cunning
3. The desire to display all that has been learned
4. The desire to awe the enemy
5. The desire to play the passive role
6. The desire to get rid of whatever disease one is affected by

> "To desire is an attachment. To desire not to desire is also an attachment."

## Physical Architecture

### The On-Guard Position (Small Phasic Bent-Knee Stance)

The foundation of all JKD movement. Not a static posture but a **state of readiness**.

**SMALL**: Appropriateness — not over-extended, not insufficient. Quick steps for speed and controlled balance.

**PHASIC**: A stage in development — constantly changing, never static.

**BENT-KNEE**: Ensures readiness in motion at all times.

```
Key Elements:
- Chin tucked to collarbone (not all the way down — meet halfway)
- Lead hand: low back-fist position to shoulder height, always in subtle motion
- Rear hand: covers solar plexus, open palm facing opponent
- Rear heel raised and cocked — the "piston of the fighting machine"
- Weight: slightly forward over lead leg, or balanced (context-dependent)
- Body forms straight line from lead heel to lead shoulder
```

### The Four Fundamental Footwork Moves

1. **Advancing** — Forward shuffle, quick advance, step-forward
2. **Retreating** — Backward shuffle, step-back, quick retreat
3. **Circling right** — Pivot on lead foot, maintain relative foot position
4. **Circling left** — More precise, shorter steps, safer against rear-hand threats

> "Move first the foot closest to the direction you wish to go in."

### Target Priorities (Progressive Weapons Charts)

**Primary targets** (closest, most vulnerable):
- Eyes (finger jab)
- Groin
- Knee/shin (lead leg attacks)
- Solar plexus

**Secondary targets**:
- Jaw/chin (straight lead, rear cross)
- Kidney/liver (hooks, shovel hooks)
- Floating ribs (side kicks, hooks)
- Temple (backfist, hammer blows)

## Qualities of the Fighter

### Coordination
Learning coordination is a matter of **training the nervous system**, not muscles. Each performance of an act strengthens neural pathways (synaptic reinforcement). Practice must be:
- Precise (not while fatigued)
- Economical (minimum energy, maximum result)
- Reflexive (progression from volition to automatic response)

### Precision
- Accuracy before speed
- Speed before power
- Mirror work for posture and position verification
- Punch through the target, not at it

### Power
Power = Force × Speed. A smaller man who swings faster may hit as hard as a heavier man who swings slowly. Power derives from:
- Waist pivot (not shoulder swing)
- Sequential body uncoiling (toes → knees → hips → shoulders → elbow → wrist)
- Last-moment acceleration (whip principle)
- Ground connection (push from earth through legs)

### Endurance
- Event-specific training (shadow boxing with broken rhythm)
- Interval training: sprints interspersed with easier running
- Minimum 6 weeks for baseline; years for peak
- Lost rapidly if not maintained

### Balance
- Feet directly under body (medium distance apart)
- Center of gravity controlled and utilized
- Body slants counter-balanced with extended limbs
- Good balance in motion, not stillness
- Never cross the feet

### Speed (Five Types)
1. **Perceptual speed** — Quickness of eye to see openings
2. **Mental speed** — Selecting the right move
3. **Initiation speed** — Economical starting from correct posture
4. **Performance speed** — Muscle contraction velocity
5. **Alteration speed** — Changing direction mid-stream

### Timing
- **Reaction time**: Stimulus → muscle movement
- **Movement time**: Execution of chosen technique
- **Broken rhythm**: Interrupting opponent's motor-set to create openings
- **Tempo**: The exact psychological and physical moment of weakness
- **Cadence**: Speed regulated to coincide with adversary's rhythm

## Tactical Framework

### Five Ways of Attack

| Way | Abbreviation | Description |
|-----|-------------|-------------|
| **Simple Angle Attack** | SAA | Attack at unexpected angle, sometimes preceded by feinting |
| **Immobilization Attack** | IA | Trapping opponent's head, hand, or leg while crashing the line |
| **Progressive Indirect Attack** | PIA | Feint or uncommitted thrust to misdirect, then hit opening line in single forward motion |
| **Attack by Combination** | ABC | Series of thrusts following naturally to multiple lines, setting up finishing blow |
| **Attack by Drawing** | ABD | Luring opponent into commitment by leaving apparent opening |

### Counterattack Principles

- Counterattacking is the **greatest art in fighting** — requires exact timing, unerring judgment, cool poise
- The counter-fighter has advantage: the attacker cannot help but expose targets
- Conditioning: specific stimulus → specific reaction (automatic, almost unconscious)
- Fighting done with the **head**, not hands or feet

### Distance Management

**Fighting measure**: The distance at which opponent cannot hit without lunging, but you can regain hitting distance with short advance.

- Constantly gain and break ground
- Lull opponent, then attack suddenly
- Attack where opponent **will be** when he realizes he's being attacked, not where he is
- Use opponent's advance against him (stop-hits, counter-time)

## Spiritual Cultivation

### Zen in Combat

> "The consciousness of self is the greatest hindrance to the proper execution of all physical action."

- **Voidness**: All-inclusive, having no opposite, nothing excluded
- **No-mind**: Action without actor, experience without experiencer
- **Purposelessness**: Spirit harbors nothing, not tipped in any direction
- **Fluidity**: Mind moves with ten thousand situations, remains ever the same

### The Path to Truth

1. Seeking after truth
2. Awareness of truth (and its existence)
3. Perception of truth (substance and direction)
4. Understanding of truth (TAO — not fragmented, but totality)
5. Experiencing of truth
6. Mastering of truth
7. Forgetting truth
8. Forgetting the carrier of truth
9. Return to the primal source where truth has its roots
10. Repose in the nothing

## Training Protocols

### Warm-Up Sequence
Must imitate actual combat movements:
1. Jumping jacks / running in place
2. Shoulder circling / alternate splits
3. High kicks / leg stretches
4. Waist twisting / palm curls
5. Shadow boxing (broken rhythm)

### Daily Physical Minimum
- Rope jumping (3 rounds, 3 minutes each, 1 minute rest)
- Shadow boxing (imagination work — "worst enemy" visualization)
- Footwork drills (all four directions, with tools)
- Heavy bag (precision, power, broken rhythm)
- Light sparring (apply principles, not techniques)

### Mental Training
- Meditation: realize imperturbability of original nature
- Visualization: opponent attacking while sitting, standing, lying down
- Study: Confucius, Spinoza, Krishnamurti, Taoist texts
- Self-inquiry: "What is the root of my movement?"

## Warnings and Prohibitions

### Do Not
- Practice fine skills while fatigued (substitutes gross for fine)
- Telegraph punches (no wind-up, no preparatory movement)
- Cross the feet (destroys balance base)
- Chase the opponent (let him come to you)
- Commit arms too deeply (difficult recovery)
- Rely on one takedown (be ready for other openings)
- Become attached to any technique ("the tool is a tool of formless form")

### Organized Despair (Anti-Patterns)
- Stylized forms that distort and cramp
- Flowery techniques that simulate but do not actualize combat
- Super mental power and spiritual abstraction that drifts into mystery
- Blind devotion to systematic uselessness of practicing routines
- The classical man: a bundle of routine, ideas, and tradition
